01 — The Concept

A room you feel your way out of.

VOID is a multi-floor escape room experience where every spatial decision is a sensory statement. The exterior is deliberately understated — no signage screaming "escape room," no neon lights. Just a door. The nonchalance is the first clue.

Once inside, the rules of normal space collapse. Furniture is exaggerated and theatrical — oversized chairs you sink into, undersized tables that demand you crouch. Nothing is comfortable in the conventional sense, but everything is intentional. You are inside an experience, not a room.

Each floor requires solving before the next can be accessed. The final escape involves crossing a water body via stepping stones — a full-body, sensory-rich culmination of the journey.

02 — Sensory Design Decisions

Every detail triggers something.

🔴 01

The Red Carpet Entry

A blood-red carpet runs from the entrance door into the reception area — theatrical and cinematic, like stepping onto a stage you didn't audition for. It signals importance and raises heart rate before anything has even happened.

Trigger: Heightened anticipation · Colour psychology · Status signal
🪑 02

Exaggerated Furniture

Nothing in VOID is normal-sized. Chairs are plush and oversized — you feel small. Tables are wrong-proportioned. The storage stools are cushioned and low, forcing you to bend down, slow down, and transition mindfully from the outside world into the experience.

Trigger: Disorientation · Scale perception · Ritual of preparation
👟 03

The Shoe Removal Ritual

The storage space near the entrance requires guests to remove shoes and store belongings before ascending. This is not just practical — it is a deliberate ritual transition. Removing shoes changes posture, pace, and psychological state. You feel the floor differently. You are now committed.

Trigger: Tactile barefoot sensation · Psychological threshold crossing
🌊 04

Water Body + Stepping Stones

The second floor's defining feature is a shallow water body with stepping stones. To solve the final clue, players must physically cross it. Cold water, the sound of splashing, the need for balance — every sense is engaged simultaneously. The clue and the escape are earned through the body, not just the mind.

Trigger: Touch · Sound · Balance · Temperature · Full-body engagement
🚪 05

Progressive Door Unlocking

No floor can be accessed until the previous one is solved. The staircase is the reward. Each door that unlocks delivers a micro-burst of dopamine — a physical click, a change in lighting, a breath of different air. Progression is made visceral, not abstract.

Trigger: Auditory click · Visual light shift · Reward feedback loop
📺 06

The Watching Space

The reception area has a TV and sofa — but they serve the world-building. CCTV feeds play in the staff-only ground floor room, while players experience the illusion of being in an unmonitored environment. The tension of being watched — or not — is a psychological layer that enriches every decision made upstairs.

Trigger: Psychological surveillance · Paranoia · Trust dynamics
03 — User Flow

How you move through VOID.

Step 01 Ground Floor · Exterior

Arrive at the Unmarked Door

The exterior gives nothing away. No branding, no noise, no signage. The nonchalance is the first sensory signal — curiosity and slight unease are triggered before stepping inside.

Sensory cue: Visual restraint · Anticipation architecture
Step 02 Ground Floor · Reception

Enter the Reception Area

Red carpet. Sofa. TV. A reception desk. It feels like a lobby but something is slightly off — the furniture is too big, the proportions wrong. Players check in here. The door to the staff room is visible but locked to them.

Sensory cue: Red carpet · Scale distortion · Visual intrigue
Step 03 Ground Floor · Storage

Remove Shoes · Store Belongings

Players move to the storage room. Cushioned stools invite them to sit, remove shoes, and store everything they brought. The ritual slows the body and transitions the mind. You are no longer a visitor — you are a participant.

Sensory cue: Tactile barefoot · Cushion softness · Psychological threshold
Step 04 Ground Floor → First Floor

Climb the Staircase

The main staircase leads to the first floor. Ascending barefoot on a staircase is a different sensation — the texture underfoot, the slight chill, the change in air. The game has already begun.

Sensory cue: Temperature shift · Surface texture · Spatial ascent
Step 05 First Floor · Level 1

Solve Level One

The first floor contains Level 1 — a room with clues, objects, and challenges to solve. The space is filled with exaggerated props and deliberate sensory design. Players cannot access the second floor until this level is solved.

Sensory cue: Visual overstimulation · Deliberate clutter · Discovery tension
Step 06 First Floor → Second Floor

Access Opens — Hidden Passage to Floor 2

Once solved, a passage inside the first floor room unlocks — bypassing the main staircase. This is deliberate: the second floor access feels secret, earned, and slightly transgressive. A different door, a different route.

Sensory cue: Auditory unlock · Surprise spatial reveal · Reward dopamine
Step 07 Second Floor · Level 2

Cross the Water · Solve the Final Clue

The second floor features a shallow water body with stepping stones. Players must physically cross it to reach the final clue — barefoot, balancing, feeling cold water, hearing splashing. Once the clue is solved, the main exit door unlocks. Escape is complete.

Sensory cue: Touch · Sound · Cold · Balance · Full physical immersion
Step 08 Second Floor → Ground Floor

Descend · Collect · Exit

With the main door open, players take the main staircase back down to the storage area to collect their shoes and belongings. The descent is calm, unhurried — a decompression. Then they exit through the front door. The nonchalant exterior greets them again, differently this time.

Sensory cue: Physical decompression · Re-orientation · Emotional afterglow
Staff Only All Floors · Staff System

Parallel Staff Infrastructure

A separate door in the reception leads to a staff-only area with a lift accessible across all floors. The ground floor staff room connects to a CCTV monitoring room. In emergencies — injury, distress — staff can use the lift to access any floor instantly without disrupting the player experience.

UX consideration: Safety access · Emergency protocol · Invisible operations layer

Red — the activator

Used for entry carpets, alert zones, unlock moments, and all CTA elements. Red raises the pulse. It marks what matters.

06 — Key Design Decisions

Why everything is the way it is.

Spatial UX

The Exterior Reveals Nothing

An escape room that announces itself undermines its own premise. VOID's exterior is deliberately low-key — no theming, no signage beyond a door number. The first psychological game begins before you enter. Anticipation is a design material.

Interaction Design

Progressive Access Over Linearity

Each floor unlocking only after solving the previous creates genuine stakes. The hidden passage from Floor 1 to Floor 2 — bypassing the main staircase — makes progress feel transgressive and secret. The architecture rewards you, not just the puzzle answer.

Sensory Design

Exaggerated Furniture as Disorientation Tool

When everything is the wrong size, your brain can't go on autopilot. Oversized chairs and misscaled objects force attention. You cannot move through this space habitually — you must be present. This is the core UX principle: design that demands engagement.

Safety UX

The Invisible Staff Layer

The entire staff system — lift, CCTV room, separate access — operates invisibly. Players never feel monitored despite being constantly safe. This dual-layer design (player experience vs. operational infrastructure) is a core UX architecture decision: safety must not break immersion.
